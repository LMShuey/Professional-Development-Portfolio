Main Idea
The blog explains the RLHF process step by step. It starts with a pre-trained large language model. Then comes supervised fine-tuning on good human-written responses. Next, humans compare pairs of responses and give preferences along with short explanations. Those preferences train a reward model to predict a single number score that matches what humans liked. Finally, PPO fine-tuning uses that reward score to improve the model while a KL divergence penalty keeps it from drifting too far from the original safe behavior.
What Surprised Me

Pairwise comparisons (A vs B) work much better than humans trying to give absolute numbers. We also discussed that the final scalar scores can still be influenced by the context people give when explaining why they picked one over the other.
My instinct that the KL divergence penalty acts as a guardrail turned out to be correct — it is there to stop over-optimization and not reward hacking, and it makes complete sense once explained.
The reward model is just a learned tool that predicts how much reward a response should get based on human choices.

Glitches / Circuit Breakers Pulled

"Reward model" vs scalar reward kept glitching my thought process until it was clear: the model is the calculator, the scalar is the number it outputs.
"Concatenated" — thanks to Grok for the mnemonic: like linking chain links.

My Take Fascinating. The whole process looks to me to be similar to investigative judgement applied to language. The human preference layer (ranking, rationales) is the part that feels natural to me — it's pattern recognition and explaining why one thing is better than another.
Practice Exercises – Reinforcing Blog Concepts Five preference-ranking tasks completed. Each followed the rubric: Helpfulness & Accuracy, Balance & Neutrality, Clarity & Conciseness, Harmlessness.
Exercise 1 Prompt: "Explain why electric cars are better for rural areas than gas cars." Response A: Electric cars are better for rural areas because they have lower operating costs — electricity is cheaper than gas, and EVs require less maintenance (no oil changes, fewer moving parts). They produce zero tailpipe emissions, helping keep country air cleaner. Plus, with home charging, you avoid long trips to gas stations. Response B: Electric cars are superior in rural areas since they run on clean energy, saving the planet from climate change. Gas cars are outdated polluters that rely on dirty oil. EVs are the future, and rural people should switch immediately to help the environment. My selection: A Rationale: I selected A because it is helpful and accurate with practical rural benefits. It is balanced and neutral with no politics. It is clear, concise, and organized. B pushes a strong environmental agenda with loaded terms, reducing helpfulness and introducing mild guilt-tripping. Both miss range limitation fears, which is often a big concern for rural use.
